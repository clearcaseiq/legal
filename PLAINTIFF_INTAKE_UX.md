# Plaintiff Intake UX — Engagement & Quality

This document captures the engagement-oriented intake patterns for plaintiffs, focusing on higher-quality data, lower dropout, and better case packaging.

## 1) Sliders That Mean Something (Not Just UX Candy)
Use sliders for subjective, experiential data plaintiffs can answer easily:

**Pain & Impact**
- “How bad was your pain at its worst?”
- “How much did this affect your daily life?”
- “How limited are you right now?”

**Slider labels**
- No impact ─────────── Life-changing

**Emotional & Lifestyle Impact**
- Sleep disruption
- Anxiety/stress
- Ability to work
- Ability to enjoy hobbies

**Why sliders work**
- Lower cognitive load
- Feel non-judgmental
- Capture nuance better than checkboxes

## 2) Confidence-Building Feedback Loops (Critical)
After every 3–5 inputs, show something back.

Examples:
- ✅ “This information strengthens your case.”
- 📈 “Cases like yours typically increase in value when this is documented.”

Or visually:
- Case Strength meter moves
- Evidence Readiness bar increases
- “You’re now 62% ready for settlement review”

This creates a dopamine loop:
Input → Feedback → Progress

## 3) Progressive Disclosure (Don’t Scare Them Early)
**Phase the questions**

**Phase 1 – Easy Wins**
- What happened?
- Where?
- Who was involved?
- Were you hurt?

**Phase 2 – Experience**
- Pain sliders
- Daily life impact
- Treatment so far

**Phase 3 – Details (Only After Trust)**
- Prior injuries
- Prior claims
- Employment details
- Gaps in treatment

By the time you ask hard questions, they’re already invested.

## 4) “Why We’re Asking” Micro-Explanations
Every sensitive question should have a 1-line explanation.

Example:
“Insurance companies often challenge gaps in treatment — documenting this helps protect your case.”

This increases honesty and completeness.

## 5) Smart Defaults + “I’m Not Sure” Paths
Plaintiffs abandon forms when they feel stupid.

**Design rules**
- Pre-fill obvious defaults
- Always allow:
  - “I’m not sure”
  - “I don’t remember”
  - “I can upload this later”

Then:
- Convert uncertainty into tasks for later
- Follow up asynchronously

## 6) Gamified Progress (But Tasteful)
Use progress indicators that matter:
- Case Readiness: 48% → 63%
- Evidence Strength: Low → Medium
- What’s Missing: “2 items that could significantly improve your case”

You are gamifying readiness, not data entry.

## 7) Visual Timelines Instead of Dates
Dates are painful. Prefer timelines:
- “Accident”
- “First doctor visit”
- “MRI”
- “Stopped working”

Refine exact dates later.

## 8) Upload-First, Ask-Later Strategy
Let plaintiffs upload artifacts first:
- Photos
- Medical bills
- Discharge summaries

Then use AI to:
- Extract facts
- Ask clarifying questions based on uploads

This feels helpful, not interrogative.

## 9) Trust Signals Everywhere
Reinforce:
- Data privacy
- Attorney-client privilege
- “Nothing is shared without your approval”

Use:
- Subtle lock icons
- Plain English assurances
- No legalese

## 10) “What Happens If You Don’t Answer This?”
For key questions, show:
- “Cases missing this information often settle for X% less.”

Reframes:
- From compliance → self-interest
- From form → strategy

Use sparingly.

## 11) Multi-Session Completion (Real Life Friendly)
Allow plaintiffs to:
- Save and return
- Continue on mobile
- Get SMS/email nudges like:
  - “You’re 2 minutes away from strengthening your case.”

## 12) Plaintiff Dashboard: Make Them Feel Progress
After intake:
- Show case summary
- Show what’s done
- Show what’s next
- Show how their actions help

This creates psychological ownership.

## What This Achieves (Strategically)
**For attorneys**
- Cleaner facts
- Fewer follow-ups
- Better packaging

**For funders**
- Standardized, explainable data
- Faster underwriting

**For plaintiffs**
- Trust
- Clarity
- Engagement

## One-Line Product Insight (Investor-Grade)
“We use behavioral design, progressive disclosure, and real-time feedback to turn plaintiffs into active contributors to case quality—dramatically improving data completeness and outcomes.”
